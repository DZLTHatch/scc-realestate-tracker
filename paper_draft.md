Executive Summary
This report examines rental market behavior across three inland California metros - Sacramento, Stockton, and Fresno - from January 2015 through May 2026. That window covers a complete Federal Reserve rate cycle: near-zero rates following the 2008 financial crisis, a gradual tightening phase from 2015 to 2018, a crash back to zero during COVID-19 in 2020, the most aggressive rate hike campaign in forty years from 2022 to 2023, and a new cutting cycle beginning in 2024. The goal was to measure how two macroeconomic variables - the Federal Funds Rate (FFR) and the Consumer Price Index (CPI) - relate to asking rents as tracked by the Zillow Observed Rent Index (ZORI).
Data came from two sources: the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) API for FFR and CPI, and Zillow Research for ZORI. The analysis ran Pearson correlation tests, multiple linear regression, first difference regression, and lag correlation testing across all three markets.
Four findings came out of the data. First, CPI tracks rent much more closely than FFR does across all three cities, with CPI-ZORI correlations around 0.96 compared to FFR-ZORI correlations around 0.71. Second, the regression models that appear strong on the surface - R² values between 0.93 and 0.97 - collapse to between 0.01 and 0.07 after first differencing, which means the apparent relationship is mostly a product of both variables trending upward over the same decade rather than one actually driving the other. Third, Fresno responds to rate increases differently than Sacramento and Stockton do. When FFR rises, Fresno rents tend to tick upward in the short term rather than fall, likely because rising mortgage rates push working-class households out of homeownership and into rentals, temporarily lifting rental demand. Fourth, FFR changes take about six months to show up in Sacramento and Stockton rents, and about twelve months in Fresno.
For investors watching the Sacramento metro, the current rate-cutting environment - FFR down from 5.33% in 2023 to 3.63% as of mid-2026 - points toward upward rent pressure in Sacramento and Stockton within the next six months, and in Fresno within twelve, if the historical pattern holds. That said, CPI trajectory will probably matter more than Fed policy alone, and any macro correlation in this market needs to be read carefully given the role of Bay Area migration, housing supply constraints, and local demographics in shaping what rents actually do.

Section 2 -- Introduction
Sacramento makes for a useful anchor market in this analysis for two reasons. First, as California's state capital, it has a stable government employment base that keeps resident turnover low and demand for housing relatively consistent even when private sector conditions shift. Second, Sacramento is affordable by California coastal standards. Compared to San Francisco, Los Angeles, and San Diego, Sacramento offers lower housing costs while still providing access to major employment centers, highways, and urban amenities. That affordability gap has pulled a steady stream of Bay Area households inland over the past decade, adding demand pressure to a housing market that was already supply-constrained.
The 2015 to 2026 time window was chosen because it captures a full Federal Reserve rate cycle within a single analytical frame. FFR started near zero, climbed gradually through 2018, dropped back to zero at the start of the pandemic, surged to 5.33% by mid-2023 in the fastest tightening campaign since the 1980s, and has since been cut back down to 3.63%. Having both a rising and falling rate environment in the same dataset makes it possible to compare rental market behavior on both sides of the cycle rather than drawing conclusions from just one direction of movement.
Three research questions guide the analysis:
RQ1: To what extent do FFR and CPI correlate with ZORI across Sacramento, Stockton, and Fresno from 2015 to 2026?
RQ2: Are those correlations capturing a real relationship between macroeconomic conditions and rent, or are they driven by the fact that all three variables happened to trend upward over the same decade?
RQ3: How long does it take for FFR changes to show up in asking rents, and does that delay differ across market types?

Section 3 -- Literature Review
3.1 How Monetary Policy Reaches Housing Markets
Mishkin (2007) maps out the path that interest rate changes travel before reaching housing markets. The direct channels include changes to the cost of borrowing for homebuyers, shifts in expectations about future home prices, and effects on new housing supply. The indirect channels work through wealth effects and credit conditions that influence both consumer spending and housing demand. What matters most for this study is Mishkin's observation that these channels operate at different speeds. Some effects hit quickly, others take months or years to fully transmit. That speed variation lines up with the lag structure found here, where FFR changes took six months to show up in Sacramento and Stockton rents and twelve months in Fresno.
Goodhart's work on monetary transmission lags adds that the broader economy does not respond to rate changes on any fixed schedule. The lag between a Fed decision and its real-world effects is variable and context-dependent. For rent specifically, this means that measuring FFR and ZORI in the same month will consistently understate the true relationship between them, because the effect has not yet had time to work through the economy.
3.2 Cap Rates and Property Valuation
Zhou (2001) examines how cap rates behave across different commercial property types, finding that volatility varies significantly by asset class. The underlying valuation logic is straightforward: a property's value equals its net operating income divided by the cap rate. When interest rates rise, investors require higher returns to justify taking on real estate risk relative to safer alternatives like government bonds. That higher required return pushes cap rates up, which mathematically compresses property values even if the building's income has not changed. This mechanism is the theoretical bridge between Fed policy and real estate pricing that motivates the empirical analysis in this paper.
3.3 Affordability, Migration, and Rental Demand
Trimbath and Montoya (2002) frame housing affordability as a three-way balance between price, income, and interest rates. When one of those three shifts sharply, the others have to compensate or demand breaks down. The 2022 to 2023 rate hike cycle is a direct application of their model: mortgage rates jumped, income did not keep pace, and a portion of would-be homebuyers stayed renters instead. That shift in tenure choice adds demand to the rental market. In Fresno specifically, where more households sit near the edge of homeownership affordability, that added rental demand appears to have temporarily pushed rents upward even as rising rates were suppressing investor activity - which is the likely explanation for the positive short-term FFR-ZORI relationship found in that market.
Johnson (2015) zeroes in on Sacramento's specific supply problem. Through interviews with local officials and a review of land use policy, he finds that housing production in the Sacramento region is constrained not just by financing costs but by restrictive zoning, Proposition 13's effects on municipal revenue incentives, and organized resistance from existing residents who benefit from limiting new development. The practical implication is that Sacramento rents face structural upward pressure regardless of what the Fed does, because the supply side of the market cannot easily expand to meet demand even when conditions would otherwise support new construction.
Johnson, Reed, and Hayes (2008) document how coastal California's affordability problems drive population toward inland metros. Sacramento sits at the receiving end of that migration flow, absorbing households priced out of the Bay Area who still want proximity to Northern California's job market. That demand is sticky - people who move to Sacramento for affordability reasons tend to stay, which means rental demand in Sacramento responds more to Bay Area housing dynamics than to local economic cycles alone.
3.4 The Spurious Correlation Problem in Time Series Data
Johansen (2007) provides the statistical grounding for one of the central methodological concerns in this paper. When two variables both trend in the same direction over time, the correlation between them will appear strong even if no real relationship exists. Johansen shows formally that in nonstationary time series - series with persistent upward or downward trends - standard correlation and regression coefficients cannot be taken at face value. The regression results in Section 6 bear this out directly: level regressions produce R² values between 0.93 and 0.97, but after first differencing to remove shared trends, those values fall to between 0.01 and 0.07. Johansen's framework is the theoretical justification for why that first difference step was necessary.
3.5 CPI Shelter and Market Rent Lags
Ball and Koh (2025) identify three reasons why the CPI shelter component lags behind market rents: most leases lock in rent for twelve months, landlords tend to smooth rent increases for renewing tenants rather than adjusting to market rates immediately, and CPI shelter is calculated using rent changes measured over six-month windows rather than month to month. These structural frictions mean that even when market rents move quickly, the official inflation measure for housing costs moves slowly.
This distinction matters for interpreting the strong CPI-ZORI correlation found in this study. The analysis uses overall CPI - the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers across all spending categories - rather than the CPI shelter sub-component specifically. The 0.96 correlation likely reflects both the shelter cost component and the broader pass-through of inflation into landlord operating costs like insurance, property taxes, and maintenance. The lag structure Ball and Koh identify at the shelter level is consistent with the six to twelve month FFR-ZORI transmission lags found in Section 6, suggesting that lease cycle friction is part of what delays the macroeconomic signal from reaching asking rents.
