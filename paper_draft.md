Executive Summary
This report examines rental market behavior across three inland California metros - Sacramento, Stockton, and Fresno - from January 2015 through May 2026. That window covers a complete Federal Reserve rate cycle: near-zero rates following the 2008 financial crisis, a gradual tightening phase from 2015 to 2018, a crash back to zero during COVID-19 in 2020, the most aggressive rate hike campaign in forty years from 2022 to 2023, and a new cutting cycle beginning in 2024. The goal was to measure how two macroeconomic variables - the Federal Funds Rate (FFR) and the Consumer Price Index (CPI) - relate to asking rents as tracked by the Zillow Observed Rent Index (ZORI).
Data came from two sources: the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) API for FFR and CPI, and Zillow Research for ZORI. The analysis ran Pearson correlation tests, multiple linear regression, first difference regression, and lag correlation testing across all three markets.
Four findings came out of the data. First, CPI tracks rent much more closely than FFR does across all three cities, with CPI-ZORI correlations around 0.96 compared to FFR-ZORI correlations around 0.71. Second, the regression models that appear strong on the surface - R² values between 0.93 and 0.97 - collapse to between 0.01 and 0.07 after first differencing, which means the apparent relationship is mostly a product of both variables trending upward over the same decade rather than one actually driving the other. Third, Fresno responds to rate increases differently than Sacramento and Stockton do. When FFR rises, Fresno rents tend to tick upward in the short term rather than fall, likely because rising mortgage rates push working-class households out of homeownership and into rentals, temporarily lifting rental demand. Fourth, FFR changes take about six months to show up in Sacramento and Stockton rents, and about twelve months in Fresno.
For investors watching the Sacramento metro, the current rate-cutting environment - FFR down from 5.33% in 2023 to 3.63% as of mid-2026 - points toward upward rent pressure in Sacramento and Stockton within the next six months, and in Fresno within twelve, if the historical pattern holds. That said, CPI trajectory will probably matter more than Fed policy alone, and any macro correlation in this market needs to be read carefully given the role of Bay Area migration, housing supply constraints, and local demographics in shaping what rents actually do.

Section 2 -- Introduction
Sacramento makes for a useful anchor market in this analysis for two reasons. First, as California's state capital, it has a stable government employment base that keeps resident turnover low and demand for housing relatively consistent even when private sector conditions shift. Second, Sacramento is affordable by California coastal standards. Compared to San Francisco, Los Angeles, and San Diego, Sacramento offers lower housing costs while still providing access to major employment centers, highways, and urban amenities. That affordability gap has pulled a steady stream of Bay Area households inland over the past decade, adding demand pressure to a housing market that was already supply-constrained.
The 2015 to 2026 time window was chosen because it captures a full Federal Reserve rate cycle within a single analytical frame. FFR started near zero, climbed gradually through 2018, dropped back to zero at the start of the pandemic, surged to 5.33% by mid-2023 in the fastest tightening campaign since the 1980s, and has since been cut back down to 3.63%. Having both a rising and falling rate environment in the same dataset makes it possible to compare rental market behavior on both sides of the cycle rather than drawing conclusions from just one direction of movement.
Three research questions guide the analysis:
RQ1: To what extent do FFR and CPI correlate with ZORI across Sacramento, Stockton, and Fresno from 2015 to 2026?
RQ2: Are those correlations capturing a real relationship between macroeconomic conditions and rent, or are they driven by the fact that all three variables happened to trend upward over the same decade?
RQ3: How long does it take for FFR changes to show up in asking rents, and does that delay differ across market types?

Section 3 -- Literature Review
3.1 How Monetary Policy Reaches Housing Markets
Mishkin (2007) maps out the path that interest rate changes travel before reaching housing markets. The direct channels include changes to the cost of borrowing for homebuyers, shifts in expectations about future home prices, and effects on new housing supply. The indirect channels work through wealth effects and credit conditions that influence both consumer spending and housing demand. What matters most for this study is Mishkin's observation that these channels operate at different speeds. Some effects hit quickly, others take months or years to fully transmit. That speed variation lines up with the lag structure found here, where FFR changes took six months to show up in Sacramento and Stockton rents and twelve months in Fresno.
Goodhart's work on monetary transmission lags adds that the broader economy does not respond to rate changes on any fixed schedule. The lag between a Fed decision and its real-world effects is variable and context-dependent. For rent specifically, this means that measuring FFR and ZORI in the same month will consistently understate the true relationship between them, because the effect has not yet had time to work through the economy.
3.2 Cap Rates and Property Valuation
Zhou (2001) examines how cap rates behave across different commercial property types, finding that volatility varies significantly by asset class. The underlying valuation logic is straightforward: a property's value equals its net operating income divided by the cap rate. When interest rates rise, investors require higher returns to justify taking on real estate risk relative to safer alternatives like government bonds. That higher required return pushes cap rates up, which mathematically compresses property values even if the building's income has not changed. This mechanism is the theoretical bridge between Fed policy and real estate pricing that motivates the empirical analysis in this paper.
3.3 Affordability, Migration, and Rental Demand
Trimbath and Montoya (2002) frame housing affordability as a three-way balance between price, income, and interest rates. When one of those three shifts sharply, the others have to compensate or demand breaks down. The 2022 to 2023 rate hike cycle is a direct application of their model: mortgage rates jumped, income did not keep pace, and a portion of would-be homebuyers stayed renters instead. That shift in tenure choice adds demand to the rental market. In Fresno specifically, where more households sit near the edge of homeownership affordability, that added rental demand appears to have temporarily pushed rents upward even as rising rates were suppressing investor activity - which is the likely explanation for the positive short-term FFR-ZORI relationship found in that market.
Johnson (2015) zeroes in on Sacramento's specific supply problem. Through interviews with local officials and a review of land use policy, he finds that housing production in the Sacramento region is constrained not just by financing costs but by restrictive zoning, Proposition 13's effects on municipal revenue incentives, and organized resistance from existing residents who benefit from limiting new development. The practical implication is that Sacramento rents face structural upward pressure regardless of what the Fed does, because the supply side of the market cannot easily expand to meet demand even when conditions would otherwise support new construction.
Johnson, Reed, and Hayes (2008) document how coastal California's affordability problems drive population toward inland metros. Sacramento sits at the receiving end of that migration flow, absorbing households priced out of the Bay Area who still want proximity to Northern California's job market. That demand is sticky - people who move to Sacramento for affordability reasons tend to stay, which means rental demand in Sacramento responds more to Bay Area housing dynamics than to local economic cycles alone.
3.4 The Spurious Correlation Problem in Time Series Data
Johansen (2007) provides the statistical grounding for one of the central methodological concerns in this paper. When two variables both trend in the same direction over time, the correlation between them will appear strong even if no real relationship exists. Johansen shows formally that in nonstationary time series - series with persistent upward or downward trends - standard correlation and regression coefficients cannot be taken at face value. The regression results in Section 6 bear this out directly: level regressions produce R² values between 0.93 and 0.97, but after first differencing to remove shared trends, those values fall to between 0.01 and 0.07. Johansen's framework is the theoretical justification for why that first difference step was necessary.
3.5 CPI Shelter and Market Rent Lags
Ball and Koh (2025) identify three reasons why the CPI shelter component lags behind market rents: most leases lock in rent for twelve months, landlords tend to smooth rent increases for renewing tenants rather than adjusting to market rates immediately, and CPI shelter is calculated using rent changes measured over six-month windows rather than month to month. These structural frictions mean that even when market rents move quickly, the official inflation measure for housing costs moves slowly.
This distinction matters for interpreting the strong CPI-ZORI correlation found in this study. The analysis uses overall CPI - the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers across all spending categories - rather than the CPI shelter sub-component specifically. The 0.96 correlation likely reflects both the shelter cost component and the broader pass-through of inflation into landlord operating costs like insurance, property taxes, and maintenance. The lag structure Ball and Koh identify at the shelter level is consistent with the six to twelve month FFR-ZORI transmission lags found in Section 6, suggesting that lease cycle friction is part of what delays the macroeconomic signal from reaching asking rents.

Section 4 -- Data and Methodology
4.1 Data Sources
This study draws on two publicly available data sources. Macroeconomic data - specifically the Federal Funds Rate and the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPIAUCSL) - was pulled directly from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) API maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. Rental market data came from Zillow Research in the form of the Zillow Observed Rent Index (ZORI), downloaded as a CSV file from the Zillow Research Data portal. Both datasets were filtered to cover January 2015 through May 2026, a window that captures a complete Federal Reserve rate cycle as described in Section 2.
4.2 The Zillow Observed Rent Index
ZORI measures asking rents for a fixed pool of rental units over time rather than averaging all available listings in a given month. The distinction matters. A simple monthly average of listed rents would fluctuate based on which types of units happen to be available - if more large three-bedroom apartments list in one month and more studios list the next, the average would swing without any underlying change in rent prices. ZORI controls for this by holding the unit mix constant, so changes in the index reflect actual price movement rather than shifts in what types of units are on the market. The fixed unit pool functions like a control group in an experiment - by keeping one variable constant, the index isolates the effect being measured. The three markets analyzed in this study are Sacramento, CA; Stockton, CA; and Fresno, CA, representing a range of market sizes, price points, and demographic compositions within inland California.
4.3 Data Processing
The raw Zillow file arrived in wide format, with each date occupying its own column across hundreds of columns to the right of the region identifier. This structure is readable for humans but difficult for statistical software to process efficiently. The data was converted to long format using pandas' melt function, collapsing all date columns into a single Date column with a corresponding ZORI value per row. This restructuring allowed the dataset to be filtered, sorted, and merged cleanly.
A date normalization step was required before merging the Zillow and FRED datasets. FRED dates the first day of each month (e.g., 2026-02-01) while Zillow dates the last day (e.g., 2026-01-31). Both were converted to year-month period format to align them on a shared monthly timeline without losing data.
Missing values appeared in the most recent months of the CPI series, where data had not yet been published at the time of download. A left join merge was used to preserve all Zillow rent observations regardless of whether a matching CPI value existed, leaving those cells empty rather than dropping the rows entirely. All rows with missing values in the FFR, CPI, or ZORI columns were excluded from statistical analysis.
4.4 Statistical Methods
Four analytical methods were applied sequentially, each addressing a specific research question.
Pearson correlation coefficients were calculated between ZORI and each macroeconomic variable - FFR and CPI - for all three cities. Pearson correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables, producing an r-value between -1 and +1. A value near +1 indicates that the two variables tend to move together, a value near -1 indicates they tend to move in opposite directions, and a value near 0 indicates no linear relationship. P-values were calculated alongside each r-value to determine statistical significance, with a threshold of p < 0.05 used to reject the null hypothesis of no correlation.
Multiple linear regression was then applied to model ZORI as a function of both FFR and CPI simultaneously. Running both predictors in the same model controls for the influence of each variable while estimating the other's coefficient, reducing the risk that a correlation between FFR and CPI themselves distorts the individual estimates. The regression equation took the form: ZORI = β₀ + β₁(FFR) + β₂(CPI) + ε, where β₁ and β₂ represent the estimated change in monthly asking rent associated with a one-unit increase in each predictor, holding the other constant.
First difference regression was applied to address the spurious correlation problem identified by Johansen (2007). Because ZORI, FFR, and CPI all trend upward over the 2015-2026 period, standard regression coefficients between them may reflect shared time trends rather than a genuine underlying relationship. First differencing transforms each variable into its month-over-month change - ΔZORI, ΔFFR, ΔCPI - removing the shared trend and leaving only the period-to-period variation. The same regression model was re-estimated on the differenced variables. A large drop in R² between the level and first difference models indicates that the original correlation was primarily trend-driven rather than structurally meaningful.
Lag correlation analysis was used to test whether FFR changes correlate more strongly with rent changes that occur months later rather than in the same period. Monthly FFR changes were shifted forward by 0, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months and correlated against ZORI changes at each lag interval. The lag producing the strongest statistically significant correlation for each city was interpreted as the approximate transmission delay between monetary policy decisions and rental market responses.
All data processing and statistical analysis was conducted in Python 3 using the pandas, scipy, sklearn, and matplotlib libraries. Scripts and processed data files are available at github.com/DZLTHatch/scc-realestate-tracker.

Section 5 -- Market Overview
5.1 Sacramento, CA
Sacramento's rental market is built on two demand drivers that operate somewhat independently of each other. The first is government employment. As California's state capital, Sacramento houses a large concentration of state and local government workers whose jobs are stable across economic cycles. People move to Sacramento for those jobs and rent while they're there, creating a baseline demand floor that doesn't disappear when the private sector slows down. Developers have responded with ongoing residential construction throughout the study period, though Johnson (2015) documents that new supply has consistently fallen short of demand due to restrictive zoning and the perverse incentives Proposition 13 creates for municipalities to block new housing rather than approve it.
The second driver is Bay Area migration. Sacramento sits roughly 90 miles northeast of San Francisco - close enough to remain a realistic option for households priced out of coastal California but unwilling to leave the region entirely. As Bay Area rents and home prices escalated through the late 2010s and accelerated after COVID, Sacramento absorbed a growing share of those displaced households. That migration demand stacks on top of the existing government employment base, and the two sources don't respond to the same things. Government workers stay regardless of what the Fed does. Bay Area migrants respond to the relative affordability gap between Sacramento and the coast. Together they make Sacramento's rental demand unusually resilient.
5.2 Stockton, CA
Stockton occupies a similar geographic and economic position to Sacramento - inland, within reach of the Bay Area, more affordable than coastal metros. One finding from the descriptive statistics surprised the initial analysis: Stockton's mean asking rent of $1,894 over the study period exceeded Sacramento's mean of $1,805, despite Sacramento being the larger and more economically prominent city. Stockton also showed the most rent volatility of the three markets, with a standard deviation of $388, suggesting a wider range of neighborhood conditions and unit types pulling the market in different directions at any given time.
Despite that volatility, Stockton's rent trajectory tracked Sacramento's closely across the full decade. The two markets accelerated together during COVID, dipped together during the 2022-2023 rate hikes, and recovered together as rates came down. That parallel behavior is consistent with both cities drawing on the same regional demand pool and responding to the same macroeconomic signals on the same timeline - the lag analysis in Section 6 finds a six-month FFR transmission delay in both markets.
5.3 Fresno, CA
Fresno is a different kind of market. Its mean asking rent of $1,492 over the study period ran roughly $400 below Sacramento and Stockton, and its tenant base skews toward working-class and agricultural households rather than government workers or Bay Area transplants. Investor activity is more limited, which has a direct effect on how quickly rent adjusts to changing conditions. Institutional landlords and sophisticated individual investors tend to monitor macroeconomic signals actively and reprice units when conditions shift. The smaller, less financially active landlords who dominate Fresno's rental market tend to adjust more slowly and in response to what they see locally rather than what the Fed announced last month.
Fresno's standard deviation of $342 was the lowest of the three cities, and its total rent growth of $996 over the decade was the smallest as well. Neither of those facts means Fresno is economically stagnant - rents still nearly doubled from 2015 to 2026. What they mean is that Fresno's market moves more steadily and with less sensitivity to the kind of investor-driven demand shocks that caused Sacramento and Stockton to spike sharply during certain periods. That consistency makes Fresno a useful reference point for comparing how the same macroeconomic forces produce different outcomes depending on who is renting, who is investing, and how connected a market is to broader California migration patterns.
5.4 Rent Trends Across the Study Period
All three markets saw rents rise across the full 2015-2026 window, but the path each took differed at four key inflection points.
From 2015 to 2019, rents climbed gradually across all three cities. The Federal Funds Rate rose steadily from near zero to around 2.5% over those four years, but rent growth kept moving upward without any visible slowdown. Sacramento's supply constraint problem and the early stages of Bay Area migration pressure were absorbing demand faster than new housing could be built, insulating rents from the modest rate increases of that period.
COVID-19 changed the pace. When the Fed cut rates to zero in early 2020 and federal stimulus money entered the economy, rent growth in all three markets steepened noticeably. The rate of increase from 2020 through 2021 was faster than anything in the pre-pandemic period. Remote work expanded the pool of households willing to consider inland California, and low borrowing costs kept investor activity in the market high.
The 2022-2023 rate hike cycle produced the first clear divergence between the three cities. Sacramento and Stockton both saw ZORI flatten and dip as FFR climbed from zero to 5.33%. Fresno did not follow that pattern - rents there kept rising through the same period. That divergence is the central anomaly this paper examines, and the most likely explanation involves the rental demand spillover from households priced out of buying homes by rapidly rising mortgage rates, an effect that hit Fresno's tenant base harder than Sacramento's more economically diverse population.
From 2024 into 2026, all three markets resumed upward rent movement as the Fed began cutting rates. One pattern visible in the full chart is that Stockton's ZORI, which was nearly identical to Sacramento's in 2015, has pulled noticeably ahead by 2026. The gap widened most during the COVID acceleration period and has not closed since. What drove Stockton's relative outperformance is not fully explained by the macroeconomic variables in this study - it likely involves submarket-level supply and demand dynamics that aggregate ZORI data cannot capture, and would be worth examining in future research with more granular data.
